Estate Planning and Wealth Transfer Guide

Estate planning is the process of arranging for the management and distribution of your assets during your lifetime and after your death. Without a formal plan, state intestacy laws will determine how your estate is divided, which may not align with your wishes. A comprehensive estate plan typically includes a will, one or more trusts, powers of attorney, healthcare directives, and beneficiary designations. According to a 2023 survey by Caring.com, only about 34% of American adults have a will, leaving the majority of families exposed to unnecessary legal complications, delays, and tax burdens. Beginning the planning process early — ideally as soon as you accumulate meaningful assets or start a family — gives you the greatest flexibility and the most options for minimizing costs and taxes.

Understanding the federal estate tax exemption is a critical starting point for any wealth transfer strategy. As of 2024, the federal estate tax exemption stands at $13.61 million per individual, or $27.22 million for a married couple using portability. Estates exceeding these thresholds are taxed at a top rate of 40%. Importantly, the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act provisions that doubled the exemption are scheduled to sunset on December 31, 2025, potentially reducing the exemption to approximately $7 million per individual (adjusted for inflation). This creates a narrow window for high-net-worth individuals to take advantage of the elevated exemption through gifting strategies before the deadline. Many states also impose their own estate or inheritance taxes with much lower thresholds — for example, Oregon and Massachusetts tax estates above $1 million — so state-level planning is equally important.

Annual gifting is one of the simplest and most effective tools for reducing a taxable estate over time. The annual gift tax exclusion allows you to give up to $18,000 per recipient in 2024 without filing a gift tax return or reducing your lifetime exemption. A married couple can combine their exclusions to give $36,000 per recipient per year. For example, a couple with three adult children and six grandchildren could transfer up to $324,000 annually free of gift tax. Over a 10-year period, that amounts to $3.24 million removed from the taxable estate. In addition to annual exclusion gifts, you may pay tuition or medical expenses directly to the institution or provider on behalf of any individual in unlimited amounts without gift tax consequences, a strategy known as direct payment exclusion.

Trusts are versatile legal instruments that can serve multiple estate planning objectives simultaneously, including probate avoidance, asset protection, tax minimization, and controlled distribution to beneficiaries. A Revocable Living Trust allows you to maintain control of your assets during your lifetime while ensuring a seamless transfer at death without probate, which can cost 3% to 7% of the gross estate value and take 12 to 24 months to complete. An Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) removes life insurance proceeds from your taxable estate — a $2 million policy owned by an ILIT rather than the insured keeps those proceeds entirely outside the estate. A Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT) allows one spouse to make a completed gift to an irrevocable trust for the benefit of the other spouse and descendants, effectively locking in today's high exemption while retaining indirect access to the assets.

Charitable giving strategies can simultaneously reduce estate taxes, provide income, and fulfill philanthropic goals. A Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT) allows you to transfer appreciated assets — such as stock with a cost basis of $50,000 now worth $500,000 — into the trust, receive an immediate partial charitable deduction, avoid capital gains tax on the sale, and receive an income stream for life or a term of up to 20 years. The remaining assets pass to your chosen charity at the end of the trust term. Alternatively, a Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) lets you make a lump-sum contribution, receive an immediate deduction of up to 60% of adjusted gross income for cash contributions, and recommend grants to charities over time. For estates subject to both income tax and estate tax, a DAF or charitable bequest can be particularly efficient because charities pay neither tax on inherited assets.

Business owners face unique estate planning challenges because closely held business interests are often illiquid and difficult to value. A Family Limited Partnership (FLP) or Family Limited Liability Company (FLLC) allows business owners to transfer interests to family members at discounted values — typically 20% to 40% below fair market value — due to lack of marketability and minority interest discounts. For example, a business valued at $5 million transferred through an FLP might be discounted to an effective transfer value of $3 million to $4 million, significantly reducing gift and estate tax exposure. A buy-sell agreement funded with life insurance ensures that a deceased owner's interest can be purchased at a predetermined price, providing liquidity for the estate and continuity for the business. Succession planning should begin at least 5 to 10 years before an anticipated ownership transition to allow time for tax-efficient restructuring.

Beneficiary designations and account titling are often overlooked but critically important components of an estate plan. Assets held in retirement accounts such as IRAs and 401(k)s, life insurance policies, annuities, and payable-on-death bank accounts pass directly to named beneficiaries, completely bypassing your will. This means that an outdated beneficiary designation — for example, naming an ex-spouse or a deceased parent — can override even a carefully drafted will. You should review all beneficiary designations at least every three years and after every major life event such as marriage, divorce, birth of a child, or death of a beneficiary. Naming a trust as beneficiary of a retirement account requires careful planning to avoid accelerating income tax, particularly following the SECURE Act of 2019, which eliminated the stretch IRA for most non-spouse beneficiaries and requires full distribution within 10 years.

Powers of attorney and healthcare directives are essential documents that address incapacity rather than death, yet they are equally important to a complete estate plan. A Durable Financial Power of Attorney designates a trusted individual to manage your financial affairs if you become unable to do so, preventing the need for a costly and time-consuming court-supervised guardianship or conservatorship proceeding. A Healthcare Power of Attorney and a Living Will (also called an Advance Healthcare Directive) specify your medical treatment preferences and designate someone to make healthcare decisions on your behalf. Without these documents, family members may disagree about your care, and courts may need to intervene. These documents should be reviewed and potentially re-executed every five to seven years, as some financial institutions and healthcare providers may be reluctant to honor documents that are more than a decade old.

Implementing and maintaining an estate plan is an ongoing process, not a one-time event. Tax laws, family circumstances, and asset values change over time, and your plan must evolve accordingly. As a practical action plan, start by compiling a complete inventory of all assets, liabilities, and existing documents. Next, meet with an estate planning attorney and a financial advisor to identify gaps and opportunities. Establish or update your core documents — will, trust, powers of attorney, and healthcare directives — and review all beneficiary designations. If your estate exceeds $5 million, engage a CPA or tax advisor to model the impact of the 2025 exemption sunset and consider accelerated gifting strategies before year-end 2025. Finally, communicate your plan to your executor, trustee, and key family members so they understand your intentions and know where to find important documents. A well-executed estate plan is one of the most meaningful financial gifts you can leave to the people and causes you care about most.
