Banking Regulatory Compliance Update 2024

Overview and Regulatory Landscape

The 2024 regulatory environment for financial institutions reflects a continued emphasis on systemic resilience, consumer protection, and digital asset oversight. Banking professionals must remain current with guidance issued by the Federal Reserve Board (FRB), the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC), the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC), and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB). The Basel III Endgame proposals, formally advanced by U.S. regulators in July 2023 and subject to ongoing revision through 2024, represent one of the most significant capital framework changes in over a decade. Institutions with total consolidated assets exceeding $100 billion must carefully monitor the finalized risk-based capital requirements, including revised treatments for operational risk, credit valuation adjustment (CVA) risk, and market risk under the Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB). Compliance officers are advised to conduct gap analyses against current capital positions no later than Q2 2024 to allow adequate remediation time before anticipated effective dates.

Bank Secrecy Act and Anti-Money Laundering Updates

The Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020 (AMLA 2020), enacted as part of the National Defense Authorization Act, continues to drive significant operational changes in 2024. The Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) has finalized the Beneficial Ownership Information (BOI) reporting rule under 31 CFR Part 1010, requiring covered companies formed or registered on or after January 1, 2024, to file beneficial ownership reports within 90 days of formation. Companies formed prior to January 1, 2024, must file by January 1, 2025. Financial institutions subject to the Customer Due Diligence (CDD) Rule under 31 CFR 1010.230 must update their compliance programs to align with the revised beneficial ownership framework, including procedures for verifying reported ownership data against FinCEN's secure database once access protocols are established. Additionally, FinCEN's updated guidance on correspondent banking relationships and nested accounts reinforces the expectation that institutions maintain robust transaction monitoring systems capable of detecting layering and structuring activity consistent with the FATF Recommendations and the Egmont Group standards. Institutions should review their Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) filing procedures to ensure timely submission within the mandatory 30-day window, extendable to 60 days when no suspect is identified.

Community Reinvestment Act Modernization

The joint final rule modernizing the Community Reinvestment Act (CRA), issued by the OCC, FDIC, and FRB in October 2023, takes effect on April 1, 2024, with full compliance required by January 1, 2026, for most provisions and January 1, 2027, for data collection and reporting requirements. This represents the most comprehensive overhaul of CRA regulations since 1995. The revised framework introduces a new performance evaluation structure that assesses retail lending, retail services and products, community development financing, and community development services across multiple geographic assessment areas, including facility-based assessment areas and, for larger institutions, retail lending assessment areas and outside retail lending areas. Institutions with assets exceeding $2 billion are classified as large banks and will be evaluated under all four performance tests. Intermediate banks, with assets between $600 million and $2 billion, will be evaluated under the retail lending test and the community development financing test. Compliance teams must begin mapping their current CRA data collection infrastructure to the new reporting requirements, which include expanded data fields for closed-end mortgage loans, small business loans, small farm loans, and automobile loans. Institutions are encouraged to engage with their primary federal regulator early in the implementation process to clarify examination expectations and assessment area delineation methodologies.

Consumer Financial Protection and Fair Lending

The CFPB has intensified supervisory focus on fair lending compliance in 2024, with particular attention to algorithmic underwriting models, appraisal bias, and digital redlining under the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA), 15 U.S.C. § 1691 et seq., and the Fair Housing Act (FHA), 42 U.S.C. § 3601 et seq. The CFPB's final rule implementing Section 1071 of the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act requires covered financial institutions to collect and report data on small business credit applications, including information on race, sex, and ethnicity of principal owners. Following litigation-related stays, institutions should monitor court proceedings and CFPB guidance for updated compliance deadlines applicable to their asset tier. Additionally, the CFPB's supervisory guidance on unfair, deceptive, or abusive acts or practices (UDAAP) has been expanded to encompass discriminatory conduct, even absent a specific statutory prohibition, reinforcing the need for comprehensive UDAAP risk assessments that include disparate impact analysis. Institutions offering buy-now-pay-later (BNPL) products should review the CFPB's interpretive rule issued in May 2024, which clarifies that BNPL lenders are card issuers subject to Truth in Lending Act (TILA) Regulation Z requirements, including dispute resolution and periodic statement obligations.

Digital Assets, Cryptocurrency, and Emerging Technology

Regulatory guidance on digital asset activities has continued to evolve through 2024, with joint statements from the FRB, OCC, and FDIC reinforcing that banks engaging in crypto-asset activities must notify their primary federal regulator in advance and demonstrate that such activities are conducted in a safe and sound manner. OCC Interpretive Letter 1179 and subsequent guidance clarify the conditions under which national banks and federal savings associations may provide cryptocurrency custody services, facilitate stablecoin transactions, and participate in independent node verification networks. The FRB's SR Letter 22-6 requires state member banks to provide advance notification before engaging in crypto-asset activities and to receive a supervisory non-objection. Institutions exploring tokenization of real-world assets, distributed ledger technology (DLT) for payment settlement, or artificial intelligence (AI)-driven credit decisioning must also address model risk management expectations outlined in SR Letter 11-7, as updated by the interagency guidance on model risk management issued in 2023. The use of large language models (LLMs) and machine learning in credit underwriting, fraud detection, and customer service introduces explainability and auditability requirements that compliance and risk management teams must incorporate into their model governance frameworks. Institutions are advised to establish cross-functional working groups comprising compliance, technology, legal, and risk management personnel to assess and document the regulatory implications of emerging technology deployments.

Liquidity Risk Management and Resolution Planning

The bank failures of March 2023, including Silicon Valley Bank and Signature Bank, prompted immediate regulatory responses that continue to shape compliance expectations in 2024. The FRB, OCC, and FDIC issued joint proposed guidance in July 2023 on liquidity risk management for large financial institutions, emphasizing the adequacy of contingency funding plans, the diversification of funding sources, and the operational readiness of discount window access. Institutions are expected to pre-position collateral at the Federal Reserve discount window and Federal Home Loan Banks and to conduct regular operational testing of these facilities. The FDIC has also advanced revisions to resolution planning requirements under 12 CFR Part 381 for insured depository institutions with $100 billion or more in total assets, requiring more detailed and operationally credible resolution strategies. Compliance officers at covered institutions should ensure that resolution plans are updated to reflect current organizational structures, material changes in business activities, and lessons learned from recent resolution events. The interagency liquidity guidance also reinforces the importance of intraday liquidity monitoring, stress testing under multiple scenarios including idiosyncratic and market-wide stress, and board-level oversight of liquidity risk appetite statements.

Cybersecurity and Operational Resilience

The interagency final rule on computer-security incident notification, effective May 1, 2022, and now fully embedded in examination expectations, requires banking organizations to notify their primary federal regulator as soon as possible and no later than 36 hours after determining that a notification incident has occurred. A notification incident is defined as a computer-security incident that has materially disrupted or degraded, or is reasonably likely to materially disrupt or degrade, the institution's ability to carry out banking operations, deliver banking products and services, or impact the stability of the financial sector. In 2024, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) cybersecurity disclosure rules under 17 CFR Parts 229 and 249 also apply to publicly traded banking organizations, requiring disclosure of material cybersecurity incidents on Form 8-K within four business days of determining materiality, as well as annual disclosures regarding cybersecurity risk management, strategy, and governance on Form 10-K. Institutions should align their incident response plans, escalation procedures, and board reporting frameworks with both the banking agency notification rule and the SEC disclosure requirements to avoid duplicative or inconsistent communications. The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Cybersecurity Framework 2.0, released in February 2024, provides updated guidance on governance, identification, protection, detection, response, and recovery functions that institutions are encouraged to incorporate into their enterprise risk management programs.

Examination Priorities and Practical Guidance for Compliance Officers

Federal and state banking examiners in 2024 are expected to focus on credit quality deterioration in commercial real estate portfolios, particularly office and multifamily sectors, in light of elevated interest rates and post-pandemic occupancy trends. Institutions with significant concentrations in commercial real estate should review the interagency guidance on concentrations in commercial real estate lending issued in 2006 and ensure that their concentration risk management frameworks, stress testing methodologies, and capital buffers are commensurate with the level of risk. Compliance officers should also prioritize third-party risk management in accordance with the interagency final guidance issued in June 2023, which establishes a risk-based approach to managing relationships with third parties throughout the lifecycle of the relationship, from planning and due diligence through ongoing monitoring and termination. The guidance applies to all third-party relationships and places heightened expectations on relationships involving critical activities. Practically, institutions should maintain a comprehensive third-party inventory, conduct tiered due diligence proportionate to the risk and criticality of each relationship, and ensure that contracts include provisions addressing regulatory access, audit rights, data security, business continuity, and termination. Compliance training programs should be updated to reflect all 2024 regulatory changes, and institutions are encouraged to schedule pre-examination meetings with their primary federal regulator to discuss examination scope, outstanding matters requiring attention, and the institution's self-assessment of its compliance posture. Proactive engagement with regulators, transparent communication, and a demonstrated commitment to continuous improvement remain the cornerstones of a sound compliance culture.
