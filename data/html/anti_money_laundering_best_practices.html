Anti-Money Laundering Best Practices

Document Classification: Internal Compliance Reference | Effective Date: January 1, 2024 | Review Cycle: Annual | Owner: Chief Compliance Officer

Anti-Money Laundering (AML) compliance is a foundational obligation for all financial institutions operating under U.S. federal law and international regulatory frameworks. The Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) of 1970, as amended by the USA PATRIOT Act of 2001, establishes the primary legal framework governing AML obligations in the United States. These statutes require financial institutions to implement robust internal controls, conduct ongoing customer due diligence, and report suspicious activity to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN). Internationally, the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) Recommendations provide a globally recognized standard for combating money laundering and terrorist financing. All banking professionals are expected to be familiar with these frameworks and to apply their requirements consistently in daily operations. Non-compliance may result in significant civil and criminal penalties, reputational harm, and regulatory enforcement actions including consent orders and cease-and-desist orders.

A cornerstone of any effective AML program is the implementation of a comprehensive Customer Identification Program (CIP) and Customer Due Diligence (CDD) framework, as mandated by FinCEN's CDD Final Rule (31 CFR 1010.230), which became effective May 11, 2018. Under this rule, covered financial institutions must identify and verify the identity of beneficial owners who own 25% or more of a legal entity customer, as well as one individual who controls the entity. Staff must collect, at minimum, the name, date of birth, address, and identification number for each beneficial owner. Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) must be applied to higher-risk customers, including Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs), non-resident aliens, customers operating in high-risk jurisdictions identified by FATF, and those engaged in cash-intensive businesses. EDD procedures should include source-of-funds verification, adverse media screening, and more frequent account reviews. Relationship managers and compliance officers must document all due diligence findings in the institution's core banking system and flag any inconsistencies for further review.

Transaction monitoring is a critical operational component of AML compliance and must be conducted through both automated systems and manual review processes. Financial institutions are required under the BSA to file Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs) for any cash transaction exceeding $10,000 in a single business day, whether conducted as a single transaction or as multiple transactions that the institution knows are related. Equally important is the obligation to file Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) with FinCEN within 30 calendar days of detecting a known or suspected violation, or within 60 days if no suspect can be identified. SAR filings must not be disclosed to the subject of the report, as tipping off is a federal offense under 31 U.S.C. § 5318(g)(2). Automated transaction monitoring systems should be calibrated regularly to minimize false positives while ensuring that genuine red flags — such as structuring, rapid movement of funds, unusual wire transfer patterns, and round-dollar transactions — are escalated promptly. All alerts generated by the monitoring system must be reviewed, documented, and either cleared with a written rationale or escalated to the AML compliance team.

The concept of a risk-based approach (RBA) is central to modern AML compliance and is explicitly endorsed by both FATF and FinCEN guidance. Under an RBA, institutions allocate compliance resources proportionally to the level of money laundering risk posed by customers, products, services, geographies, and delivery channels. Institutions must conduct and document a formal AML Risk Assessment at least annually, evaluating inherent risks and the effectiveness of existing controls to arrive at a residual risk rating. High-risk categories typically include private banking clients, correspondent banking relationships, money services businesses (MSBs), virtual asset service providers (VASPs), and customers in jurisdictions subject to OFAC sanctions or FATF grey-list designations. The risk assessment should inform the institution's BSA/AML Program, which must include, at minimum, the four pillars required under 31 CFR 1020.210: internal policies and procedures, a designated BSA Compliance Officer, ongoing employee training, and independent testing or audit of the program. Failure to maintain an adequate risk assessment has been cited by regulators, including the OCC and FDIC, as a leading deficiency in enforcement actions.

OFAC sanctions compliance is closely intertwined with AML obligations and must be treated as a distinct but complementary program. The Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) administers and enforces economic and trade sanctions based on U.S. foreign policy and national security goals. Financial institutions are required to screen all customers, transactions, and counterparties against OFAC's Specially Designated Nationals and Blocked Persons (SDN) List, as well as country and sector-based sanctions programs. Screening must occur at onboarding, at regular intervals during the customer relationship, and in real time for wire transfers and other payment instructions. Any match or potential match must be escalated immediately to the compliance team, and blocked or rejected transactions must be reported to OFAC within 10 business days using the appropriate reporting form. Institutions should also be aware of secondary sanctions risks and the implications of correspondent banking relationships with foreign financial institutions that may have exposure to sanctioned entities. OFAC violations can result in civil penalties up to the greater of $356,579 per violation or twice the transaction value, as well as criminal referrals to the Department of Justice.

Employee training is a mandatory element of a sound BSA/AML program and must be tailored to the roles and responsibilities of different staff categories. Front-line staff, including tellers, relationship managers, and customer service representatives, must receive training on recognizing red flags for money laundering, structuring, and fraud, as well as their obligation to escalate concerns without alerting the customer. Compliance officers and BSA staff require more advanced training covering SAR and CTR filing requirements, regulatory updates, and case study analysis. Senior management and the Board of Directors must receive periodic briefings on the institution's AML risk profile, program effectiveness, and any regulatory developments that may affect the institution's obligations. Training must be documented, including dates, attendees, content covered, and assessment results, and records must be retained for a minimum of five years in accordance with BSA recordkeeping requirements under 31 CFR 1010.430. Institutions should supplement mandatory training with scenario-based exercises, typology updates from FinCEN advisories, and participation in information-sharing programs under Section 314(b) of the USA PATRIOT Act, which allows financial institutions to voluntarily share information with one another to identify and report money laundering and terrorist financing.

Independent testing and audit of the BSA/AML program is required under federal regulation and must be conducted by qualified personnel who are independent of the compliance function. The scope of the audit should encompass all elements of the BSA/AML program, including the adequacy of policies and procedures, the effectiveness of transaction monitoring systems, the quality of SAR and CTR filings, the completeness of CIP and CDD records, and the institution's OFAC compliance program. Audit findings must be reported directly to the Board of Directors or a designated Board committee, such as the Audit Committee, and management must develop a written corrective action plan with defined timelines for remediation. Regulatory examiners from the OCC, Federal Reserve, FDIC, and NCUA, as applicable, will review audit reports and corrective action plans as part of their BSA examination procedures. Institutions that proactively identify and remediate deficiencies are generally viewed more favorably by regulators than those in which deficiencies are first identified by examiners. All audit workpapers, findings, and management responses must be retained for a minimum of five years and made available to regulators upon request.

Emerging risks and evolving typologies require financial institutions to continuously update their AML programs to address new threats. FinCEN has issued advisories on risks associated with virtual currencies and digital assets, human trafficking, cybercrime proceeds, and the exploitation of shell companies and real estate transactions for money laundering purposes. The Corporate Transparency Act (CTA), enacted as part of the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2021, further strengthens beneficial ownership transparency by requiring most U.S. legal entities to report beneficial ownership information directly to FinCEN, with a phased implementation timeline beginning January 1, 2024. Banking professionals should monitor FinCEN advisories, FATF mutual evaluation reports, and interagency guidance issued by the Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council (FFIEC) BSA/AML Examination Manual, which serves as the authoritative reference for regulatory expectations. Questions regarding the interpretation of AML requirements or the appropriate handling of specific customer situations should be directed to the institution's BSA Compliance Officer. All staff are reminded that AML compliance is not solely the responsibility of the compliance department — it is a firm-wide obligation that requires vigilance, integrity, and a commitment to protecting the financial system from criminal exploitation.
