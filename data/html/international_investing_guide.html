International Investing Guide

International investing involves allocating a portion of your portfolio to assets outside your home country, providing exposure to economies, industries, and growth opportunities that may not be available domestically. For U.S.-based investors, domestic markets represent approximately 42% of global market capitalization, meaning that a purely domestic portfolio ignores roughly 58% of the world's investable assets. By diversifying internationally, investors can potentially reduce overall portfolio volatility, capture growth in emerging markets, and hedge against domestic economic downturns. This guide will walk you through the key principles, strategies, and practical steps for building and managing an internationally diversified portfolio.

A foundational concept in international investing is geographic diversification. Financial planners commonly recommend that investors allocate between 20% and 40% of their equity holdings to international stocks. A widely cited benchmark is the Vanguard Target Retirement series, which allocates roughly 40% of its equity exposure to international equities. Within that international allocation, a typical split might be 70% to developed markets — such as Europe, Japan, Australia, and Canada — and 30% to emerging markets such as China, India, Brazil, and South Korea. For example, an investor with a $200,000 equity portfolio might hold $80,000 in international equities, with approximately $56,000 in developed markets and $24,000 in emerging markets.

Developed market investments generally offer stability and transparency comparable to U.S. markets. Countries in the MSCI EAFE Index (Europe, Australasia, and Far East) have well-established regulatory frameworks, liquid markets, and relatively low political risk. Historically, developed international markets have returned approximately 7% to 8% annually over long periods, though with periods of significant underperformance relative to U.S. markets. Low-cost index funds such as those tracking the MSCI EAFE Index typically carry expense ratios of 0.05% to 0.20%, making them an efficient vehicle for broad developed-market exposure. Investors should consider currency risk, as returns are affected by fluctuations between the U.S. dollar and foreign currencies like the euro, yen, or British pound.

Emerging market investments carry higher risk but offer the potential for greater long-term returns. Countries such as India, which has posted GDP growth rates of 6% to 8% annually in recent years, and Vietnam, with growth rates near 7%, represent economies expanding far faster than most developed nations. The MSCI Emerging Markets Index has historically delivered annualized returns of approximately 10% to 11% over multi-decade periods, though with significantly higher volatility — standard deviations of 20% to 25% compared to roughly 15% for developed markets. Investors should be prepared for drawdowns of 40% or more during periods of global stress, as seen during the 2008 financial crisis and the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic. A long investment horizon of at least 7 to 10 years is advisable before committing meaningful capital to emerging markets.

Currency risk is one of the most important factors to understand when investing internationally. When the U.S. dollar strengthens against foreign currencies, the value of international holdings decreases in dollar terms, even if the underlying assets have appreciated in local currency. For instance, if a European stock fund rises 8% in euro terms but the euro weakens 5% against the dollar, the U.S. investor's net return is only approximately 3%. Some funds offer currency-hedged versions that use forward contracts to neutralize this effect, typically at an additional cost of 0.50% to 1.00% per year. For long-term investors, many financial advisors recommend accepting currency risk as part of the diversification benefit, since over time currency fluctuations tend to average out and the hedging cost can erode returns.

Tax considerations are a critical but often overlooked aspect of international investing. The U.S. government allows investors to claim a Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) for taxes withheld by foreign governments on dividends and interest income. For example, many European countries withhold 15% to 30% on dividends paid to U.S. investors. If you hold international funds in a taxable brokerage account, you can typically claim the FTC on IRS Form 1116, effectively reducing your U.S. tax liability dollar-for-dollar. However, if you hold international funds in a tax-advantaged account such as an IRA or 401(k), you cannot claim the FTC, making taxable accounts the more tax-efficient location for international equity funds. Consulting a tax advisor familiar with international investment taxation is strongly recommended.

Practical implementation of an international strategy can be achieved efficiently through exchange-traded funds (ETFs) and mutual funds. Some widely used options include the iShares Core MSCI EAFE ETF (IEFA) with an expense ratio of 0.07%, the Vanguard FTSE Emerging Markets ETF (VWO) at 0.08%, and the iShares MSCI ACWI ex U.S. ETF (ACWX) at 0.33% for broad international exposure in a single fund. For investors who prefer active management, international mutual funds from firms such as Dodge & Cox, American Funds, and Fidelity offer professional stock selection, though typically at higher expense ratios of 0.50% to 1.00%. Dollar-cost averaging — investing a fixed amount, such as $500 per month, regardless of market conditions — is an effective strategy for building an international position over time while reducing the impact of market timing.

Rebalancing is essential to maintaining your target international allocation over time. Because different markets perform differently in any given year, your actual allocation will drift from your target. For example, if U.S. markets significantly outperform international markets for several consecutive years, as occurred from 2014 to 2021, your international allocation may shrink from a target of 30% to only 20% of your equity portfolio. A disciplined rebalancing policy — either calendar-based (annually or semi-annually) or threshold-based (rebalancing when any allocation drifts more than 5 percentage points from target) — helps ensure you are systematically buying underperforming assets at lower prices and selling outperforming assets at higher prices. Transaction costs and tax implications should be considered when rebalancing in taxable accounts.

Building a successful international investment strategy requires patience, discipline, and a clear understanding of your risk tolerance and time horizon. Begin by assessing your current portfolio to determine your existing international exposure, then set a target allocation consistent with your financial goals — commonly 20% to 40% of equities for most long-term investors. Select low-cost, diversified funds to implement your strategy, pay attention to tax placement, and commit to a regular rebalancing schedule. Avoid the temptation to abandon international holdings during periods of underperformance, as the diversification benefits are most valuable precisely when domestic and international markets diverge. Over a 20- to 30-year investment horizon, a well-constructed international allocation has historically contributed meaningfully to both risk reduction and total portfolio returns.
