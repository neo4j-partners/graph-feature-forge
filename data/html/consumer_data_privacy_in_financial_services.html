Consumer Data Privacy in Financial Services

Document Classification: Internal Compliance Reference | Effective Date: January 1, 2024 | Review Cycle: Annual | Issuing Department: Compliance and Regulatory Affairs

Consumer data privacy in financial services is governed by a complex and overlapping framework of federal and state regulations designed to protect the personal and financial information of individuals. The primary federal statute governing this area is the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA) of 1999, which requires financial institutions to explain their information-sharing practices to customers and to safeguard sensitive data. Under GLBA, institutions must provide customers with clear and conspicuous privacy notices at the time of establishing a customer relationship and annually thereafter, unless the institution qualifies for an exception under the 2018 amendment to Regulation P. The Act distinguishes between "customers," who have an ongoing relationship with the institution, and "consumers," who engage in one-time transactions, and imposes different obligations for each category. Compliance officers must ensure that all privacy notices are accurate, current, and delivered through an approved method, including electronic delivery where proper consent has been obtained in accordance with the Electronic Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act (E-SIGN Act).

The FTC Safeguards Rule, promulgated under GLBA and significantly updated in 2021 with full compliance required by June 2023, establishes specific technical and administrative requirements for protecting customer information. Financial institutions subject to FTC jurisdiction — including mortgage brokers, non-bank lenders, and certain fintech entities — must implement a comprehensive written information security program (WISP) that includes, at minimum: designation of a qualified individual to oversee the program; a risk assessment process; implementation of access controls, encryption, and multi-factor authentication; monitoring and testing of safeguards; and an incident response plan. Banking professionals responsible for vendor management must also ensure that third-party service providers with access to customer data are contractually obligated to implement equivalent safeguards, consistent with the requirements outlined in the Interagency Guidelines Establishing Information Security Standards issued jointly by the OCC, Federal Reserve, and FDIC under the authority of the Bank Service Company Act.

The Right to Financial Privacy Act (RFPA) of 1978 restricts the ability of federal agencies to obtain access to customer financial records held by financial institutions without proper legal process or customer authorization. Banking professionals must be familiar with the procedures for responding to government requests, including subpoenas, summonses, search warrants, and formal written requests. Institutions are generally required to notify customers of such requests unless a court order delays notification, and must challenge requests that do not comply with the Act's procedural requirements. Separately, the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) and its implementing regulations create a tension with privacy obligations, as institutions are required to collect, retain, and in certain circumstances report customer information to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) without customer notification. Compliance teams must maintain clear internal protocols that delineate when BSA/AML obligations supersede standard privacy protections, particularly in the context of Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs), which carry strict confidentiality requirements under 31 U.S.C. § 5318(g)(2).

State-level privacy laws have introduced additional compliance obligations that may exceed federal minimums. The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) of 2018, as amended by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA) of 2020, grants California residents broad rights including the right to know what personal information is collected, the right to delete personal information, the right to opt out of the sale or sharing of personal information, and the right to correct inaccurate data. While financial institutions subject to GLBA receive a partial exemption from CCPA for data collected and used in the context of a GLBA-covered relationship, this exemption is narrowly construed and does not cover all data held by the institution. Institutions operating in multiple states must also monitor emerging privacy legislation in states such as Virginia (Consumer Data Protection Act), Colorado (Colorado Privacy Act), Connecticut, and Texas, each of which imposes varying rights and obligations. A state-by-state compliance matrix should be maintained and updated quarterly by the Privacy Office.

The Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), enforced by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) and the FTC, imposes specific obligations on institutions that furnish data to consumer reporting agencies or use consumer reports for credit, employment, or other permissible purposes. Institutions must ensure the accuracy of information furnished to credit bureaus, investigate consumer disputes within the statutory timeframes (generally 30 days, extendable to 45 days under certain circumstances), and provide adverse action notices when a consumer report is used to take adverse action against a consumer. The Fair and Accurate Credit Transactions Act (FACTA), an amendment to FCRA, adds requirements related to identity theft prevention, including the establishment of a written Identity Theft Prevention Program (Red Flags Rule) for covered accounts. Compliance officers should ensure that Red Flags Programs are reviewed and updated at least annually and approved by the Board of Directors or a designated senior committee.

Data breach notification obligations represent a critical and time-sensitive compliance responsibility. At the federal level, the Interagency Guidelines on Response Programs for Unauthorized Access to Customer Information require FDIC-supervised institutions to notify their primary federal regulator as soon as possible and no later than 36 hours after determining that a notification incident has occurred, pursuant to the Computer Security Incident Notification Rule finalized in 2021. Additionally, institutions must notify affected customers when it is reasonably possible that the breach has resulted or will result in substantial harm or inconvenience. All 50 states, the District of Columbia, and several U.S. territories have enacted breach notification laws with varying definitions of personal information, notification timelines, and required content of notices. Institutions must maintain a breach response plan that accounts for the most stringent applicable requirements and includes pre-approved notification templates, designated legal and communications contacts, and a documented escalation matrix.

Emerging regulatory developments require ongoing vigilance from banking compliance professionals. The CFPB's rulemaking under Section 1033 of the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act — finalized in October 2024 — establishes a framework for consumer financial data rights, requiring covered institutions to make consumer financial data available to authorized third parties upon consumer request, subject to defined security and privacy standards. This open banking framework introduces new obligations around data minimization, secondary use restrictions, and third-party authorization management. Institutions should also monitor guidance from the Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC) and international frameworks such as the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) for cross-border data transfer implications affecting institutions with international operations or customers. All staff with access to customer data must complete annual privacy training, and the institution's Privacy Officer should conduct a formal Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA) for any new product, system, or process that involves the collection or processing of personal information prior to deployment.

Accountability and Governance: Ultimate responsibility for consumer data privacy compliance rests with the Board of Directors, which must receive at least annual reporting on the institution's privacy program, material incidents, and regulatory developments. The Chief Privacy Officer (CPO) or designated Privacy Officer is responsible for day-to-day program management, policy maintenance, training oversight, and regulatory liaison. Business line managers are accountable for ensuring that their operations comply with applicable privacy requirements and that any exceptions or deviations are escalated to the Compliance Department promptly. Violations of consumer data privacy regulations may result in civil money penalties, regulatory enforcement actions, private rights of action, and significant reputational harm. Questions regarding the application of this guidance to specific business activities should be directed to the Compliance and Regulatory Affairs Department before implementation.
